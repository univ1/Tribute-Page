Noam Chomsky

Linguist, Philosopher, Cognitive Scientist, Political Activist, Dissident, Historian, Author

Timeline

  • 1928 - Born in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, U.S.
  • 1939 - Wrote essay on spread of fascism following the fall of Barcelona during the Spanish Civil War
  • 1945 - Began studying philosophy, logic, and language at the University of Pennsylvania
  • 1947 - Met Zellig Harris—whom Chomsky credits with having convinced him not to drop out—in a political circle.
    Finished his BA honors thesis in linguistics
  • 1949 - Married Carol Doris Schatz, a childhood friend
  • 1951-55 - Joined Harvard Society of Fellows and further developed anarcho-syndicalist and libertarian socialist political views.
    Made significant academic contributions in mathematical linguistics, symbolic logic, and cognitive science.
  • 1952 - Published first academic article, "Systems of Syntactic Analysis" in the Journal of Symbolic Logic
  • 1955 - Received Doctor of Philosophy degree for thesis on transformational grammar, exempting him from compulsory military service.
  • 1957 - Published Syntactic Structures which was the beginning of a revolution in the field of linguistics.
    First child, Aviva is born.
  • 1959 - Arguments against B. F. Skinner's behaviorism play a major role in ushering in the age of cognitive science.
  • 1960s-70s - Faced criticism from earlier colleagues and students in a period known as the Linguistic Wars. Some former colleagues and students proposed a program which focused on semantics rather than syntax.
  • 1961 - Awarded tenure, becoming full professor at Massachusetts Institute of Technology
  • 1962 - Appointed as plenary speeaker at Ninth International Congress of Linguists in Cambridge, Massachusetts, establishing him as the most important figure in American linguistics.
    Began protesting America's involvement in the Vietnam War.
  • 1966 - Delivered lectures at the University of California, Berkeley, which were compiled and published in Language and Mind (1968)
  • 1967-75 - Participated in left-wing anti-war activism and was arrested multiple times.
    Held radical political courses at MIT despite the conservative-dominated political science department.
    Received multiple honorary doctorates for his continued work in linguistics.
  • 1967 - Cofounded Resist, a foundation that supports popular movements across the country.
    Published "The Responsibility of Intellectuals", positioning him as the nation's leading voice against U.S. involvement in the Vietnam War.
  • 1970 - Visited Southeast Asia to tour refugee camps in Laos and give lectures at Vietnam's Hanoi University of Science and Technology.
  • 1971 - Participated in famous televised debate with French post-structuralist Michel Foucault in which he laid out many of the ideas for which he came to be regarded as a figurehead in analytic philosophy.
  • 1973 - Led committee commemorating the War Resisters League's 50th anniversary.
    Coauthored Counter-Revolutionary Violence: Bloodbaths in Fact & Propaganda with Edward S. Herman. Warner Modular published the book, but its parent company ordered all copies destroyed because of its radical nature.
  • 1974 - Became a corresponding fellow of the British Academy.
  • 1977 - Appeared on television with interviewer Brian Magee, British philosopher of BBC in an interview called "The Ideas of Chomsky", in which the two explored the philosophical implications of Chomsky's linguistic theory. The most prominent ideas of Chomsky's that were highlighted in this famous interivew included the growth-like character of language acquisition, and the infinitely creative aspect of language use.
  • 1978-80 - Sparked controversy by defending a holocaust denier's—Robert Faurisson's—right to freedom of speech in an event known as the Faurisson Affair. Chomsky was misunderstood as being a holocaust denier himself because his plea for Faurisson's freedom of speech was included without his knowledge as a preface in Faurisson's book.
  • 1979 - Managed to publish Counter-Revolutionary Violence as the revised The Political Economy of Human Rights through South End Press.
  • 1983 - Argued that the Israeli-Palestinian conflict was being used by the U.S. to advance its own ends in his book The Fateful Triangle.
  • 1985 - Traveled to Managua during the Nicaraguan Contra War to give public lectures on politics and linguistics and to meet with workers' organizations and refugees.
  • 1987 - Published his Managua Lecture series in a book called On Power and Ideology.
  • 1988 - Published Manufacturing Consent, an enormously influential book which he coauthored with Herman in which the mainstream media was criticised as serving the interests of power through propaganda, filtering information, and censorship.
  • 1989 - Published Necessary Illusions: Thought Control in Democratic Societies in which he argued that countries which purport to be democratic strive to control the thoughts of their citizens—since they can't control them through force—to limit the influence they have on policy.
  • 1990s - Committed himself to the cause of East Timor independence and by publicly exposing the issues, he did more to aid in its freedom from Indonesian rule than almost any other living person.
  • 1995 - East Timorese Relief Association and the National Council for East Timorese Resistance asked Chomsky to deliver talks in Australia which were compiled as Powers and Prospects in 1996.
  • 1999 - Criticized Australian-led International Force for East Timor for exploiting Timor's independence in order to access oil and gas reserves under the Timor Gap Treaty.
  • 2001 - Was heavily outspoken against the Bush administration and the War on Terror following the September 11 attacks.
    Gave the D.T. Lakdawala Memorial Lecture in New Delhi, India.
  • 2002-3 - Supported the World Social Forum by attending its events in Brazil and India.
  • 2002 - Retired from MIT, but continued to conduct research and give lectures in his capacity as professor emeritus.
  • 2003 - Invited to Cuba by the Latin American Association of Social Scientists.
    Published Hegemony or Survival articulating America's imperial grand strategy, abuse of state power, and further critiquing the Iraq war and other aspects of the War on Terror.
  • 2011-13 - Supported the Occupy movement by chronicling its influence in Occupy (2012) and Occupy: Reflections on Class War, Rebellion and Solidarity (2013), and also by delivering talks.
  • 2014 - Became a senior fellow of the advisory council of an organization in favor of global abolition of nuclear weapons known as the Nuclear Age Peace Foundation.
  • 2016-7 - Documentary, Requiem for the American Dream outlines Chomsky's views on capitalism and economic inequality.
  • 2017-20 - Continues to be very outspoken against the Trump administration on issues ranging from Trump's climate change denial to his increasing the threat of nuclear war.
  • 2017 - Hired as part-time professor at University of Arizona in Tucson.
  • 2018 - Signed the Declaration on the Common Language of the Croats, Serbs, Bosniaks, and Montenegrins, stating that because the four peoples are mutually comprehensible and because emphasizing differences has led to segregation and discrimination, they ought to be regarded as having a common language.
Additional Information

"...in the writings of philosophers today, I should say [Chomsky's] name probably occurs as often as that of any living person." - Brian Magee, "The Ideas of Chomsky", BBC Interview, 1977

"Judged in terms of the power, range, novelty and influence of his thought, Noam Chomsky is arguably the most important intellectual alive today." - Paul Robinson, "The Chomsky Problem", The New York Times, 1979

"Noam Chomsky is recognized as the 'father of modern linguistics.' He is also one of the founders of cognitive science. He has published more than 100 books ranging across the disciplines of linguistics, philosophy, and politics." - Bill Lamb, "Biography of Noam Chomsky, Writer and Father of Modern Linguistics", ThoughtCo., 2019
